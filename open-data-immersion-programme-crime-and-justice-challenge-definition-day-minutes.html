Open Data Immersion Programme: Crime and Justice Challenge Definition Day Minutes

20 March 2013

Objectives:

  1. Explore the Crime and Justice Landscape
  2. Discuss the opportunities within the sector
  3. Define the challenges for the series

The landscape - what do we mean by Crime and Justice?

  • The Courts Service
  • CPS
  • The Judiciary
  • NOMS
  • Police
  • Rehabilitation
  • Asylum
  • Migration
  • Prisons and probation
  • Crime Stoppers
  • Victim Support
  • DVLA
  • Ordnance Survey (geo-spatial data)

The problem is that you don’t know what information/data there is.

Within the MoJ there’s a lot of good unadulterated data analysis which is done on the cause of data.

Statistics pageof the MoJ website provides a lot of good quality data.

Ordnance Survey have data that can be provided into the sector.

What other HO data would be useful?

What are the key issues in the Crime and Justice sector?

There has been fear about the impact of releasing data but it’s informing society. Getting information and data out is a good thing. There had been thought to be risks around house price reduction from knowing about crime on your street, but this hasn’t happened.

Need the context for analysis of criminal justice data.

The Home Office are looking at FOI requests to spot commonality and see if there are trends/issues that can be answered through the release of datasets.

→ Desire to demonstrate a reduction in expenditure through releasing open data which will help negate the need for FOI requests.

Total Car Check are trying to find better ways of combating car crime. Data comes from a 50 - 50 split of public/private data.

DVLA and Police hold relevant data but they do not have access to a key dataset which means they can’t launch a product which could genuinely help people.

Peripheral government agencies hold a lot of useful data which address a range of crime. This has the potential to

  • benefit the economy
  • reduce policing costs

The NPIA is planning to release further datasets.

MoJ release sentencing data at an anonymised level.

The data owners recognise that it is counterintuitive to not release datasets.

Need to be careful on the measures. e.g. how do you measure zero tolerance?

What do people want?

  • better outcomes
  • seeing where crime happens - standardising geo-coding of where crimes are done
  • when does the crime happen?
  • the punishment fits the crime
  • rehabilitation
  • am I safe?
  • what’s been done to improve the outcomes?
  • breakdown of information by population density
  • prison population although prisoners are not necessarily from the area they are imprisoned

What datasets are there?

  • Caseload data
  • Risk and Need data - factors that lead to offending
  • Re-offending data (history, demographics)
  • Prison data
  • Intervention data
  • How long it takes to deal with a case (number of days to progress)
  • Drug and alcohol abuse
  • Immigration and Asylum
  • Stolen Vehicles
  • Disclosure and Barring service (Criminal Record Bureau and Independent Safeguards Authority)
  • Passports
  • How long it takes to get a passport

What datasets are coming out?

Stolen vehicles

MoJ are currently looking at what datasets to release over the next year.

HO are looking at what they can include in their Open Data Strategy refresh

Feedback for data.gov.uk

→ It would be useful to group dataset release by month and make it searchable by department. (feedback to DGU)

→ Can DGU include a publication schedule so people can see in a central place

→ What formats are datasets available in, what (additional) processes do you need to go through to get it, is there an API? → Can DGU provide this information?

Two key areas emerging for this series:

→ Reporting of crime/cost of policing

→ Reduction of crime

Government’s responsibility is to publish the data, not to create the services.

Innovation will enable the creation of

  • Services/projects that can go back into public sector
  • Services to the public

Potential for linking datasets

‘Transparency agenda needs to make a difference to my life.’

The cost of car crime is approximately £3.5 billion but through opening up access to datasets from all the appropriate organisations then there’s potential to impact this.

Ordnance Survey can help with linked data.

Social media is key to demonstrating the connections.

However we must acknowledge that some problems can’t be solved with open data.

There are a huge number of datasets between departments which can be linked together.

The potential for linking datasets is huge but there are a lot of ways to do that very badly. Need to be very thorough on the methodology used and to take care with anonymisation.

What are the challenges?

A lot of people only have sporadic contact with the police so don’t know what they do on a day-to-day level. Challenge to increase participation in day-to-day policing.

Education - who deals with this? How do you report it? Enhancing the experience.

Examine the crime prevention/reporting/process

How effective are the sentences given in court to deliver change?

How successful are rehabilitation interventions?

Prison population data.

When and where are the places to avoid?

How to get information to people about crime so they don’t have to go looking for it? → Self service/discovery.

Reduce/prevent crime over a period of time.

Find patterns in behaviour.

Change behaviour to find benefits in communities.

Saving (money, lives, tax) and increase efficiency (reduce FOI requests)

Ensure a high level of anonymity

The Challenges

How can open data projects be constructed that achieve one of the following:

  • Increase in community involvement with the criminal justice system
  • Create further evidence for what are the right interventions for rehabilitation?
  • Address the rise in personal crime
